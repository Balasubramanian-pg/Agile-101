# Chapter 3 – Scrum Framework in Depth

A focused exploration of Scrum as one of the most widely adopted Agile frameworks. This chapter explains the roles, events, artefacts, and quality mechanisms that enable Scrum teams to deliver value predictably and collaboratively.

### [Acceptance Criteria in Agile – Defining Done and Ensuring Quality](Chapter%203%20Scrum%20Framework%20in%20Depth/Acceptance%20Criteria%20in%20Agile-%20Defining%20Done%20and%20Ensuring%20Quality.md)

This section explains how acceptance criteria translate user needs into clear, testable conditions. It describes how well-written criteria guide development, prevent ambiguity, and ensure that teams and stakeholders share the same understanding of what success looks like. The write-up also highlights how acceptance criteria support quality, alignment, and efficient testing within Scrum teams.

### [Agile Meetings and Ceremonies – The Rhythm of Agile](Chapter%203%20Scrum%20Framework%20in%20Depth/Agile%20Meetings%20and%20Ceremonies-%20The%20Rhythm%20of%20Agile.md)

This file explores the key ceremonies that create predictability and cadence in Scrum. It explains the purpose of sprint planning, daily scrum, sprint review, and sprint retrospective, showing how each event supports transparency, inspection, and adaptation. The section helps readers understand how these ceremonies maintain momentum and strengthen collaboration.

### [Definition of Done (DoD) in Agile – Ensuring Quality and Shared Understanding](Chapter%203%20Scrum%20Framework%20in%20Depth/Definition%20of%20Done%20%28DoD%29%20in%20Agile-%20Ensuring%20Quality%20and%20Shared%20Understanding.md)

This part clarifies the role of the Definition of Done as a quality baseline across the team. It explains how the DoD prevents incomplete or inconsistent work from being marked as finished, and how it promotes shared understanding, maintainability, and transparency. The write-up also discusses the impact of a strong DoD on product integrity and team accountability.

### [Readme](Chapter%203%20Scrum%20Framework%20in%20Depth/Readme.md)

This file provides an overview of how the chapter is organized. It explains how the sections on roles, ceremonies, artefacts, and quality practices come together to give a complete understanding of Scrum. It also guides readers on how to navigate the chapter depending on their familiarity with Agile practices.

### [Scrum Roles – Defining Responsibilities in Agile Teams](Chapter%203%20Scrum%20Framework%20in%20Depth/Scrum%20Roles-%20Defining%20Responsibilities%20in%20Agile%20Teams.md)

This section describes the three Scrum roles: Product Owner, Scrum Master, and Developers. It explains how each role contributes uniquely to value delivery and how clearly defined responsibilities prevent confusion and improve team cohesion. The write-up emphasizes accountability, collaboration, and the balance between autonomy and alignment.

### [Sprint Activities – The Heartbeat of Scrum](Chapter%203%20Scrum%20Framework%20in%20Depth/Sprint%20Activities-%20The%20Heartbeat%20of%20Scrum.md)

This file outlines what actually happens during a sprint, from planning to execution to inspection. It breaks down how teams manage the sprint goal, track progress, handle mid-sprint discoveries, and prepare for the final review. The section helps readers understand the continuous flow of work that makes Scrum predictable yet adaptable.

### [User Stories in Agile Development – Capturing User Needs](Chapter%203%20Scrum%20Framework%20in%20Depth/User%20Stories%20in%20Agile%20Development-%20Capturing%20User%20Needs.md)

This section explains how user stories help teams express requirements in a user-centric, outcome-focused format. It describes the structure of a good user story, how INVEST principles guide clarity, and how stories enable lightweight, flexible conversation. The write-up also discusses how stories flow through the backlog and support ongoing refinement.

> [!tip]
> This chapter builds a complete understanding of Scrum by bringing together roles, ceremonies, artefacts, and quality practices. It equips learners to see Scrum not as isolated events but as an integrated system of collaboration and continuous delivery.
